## 1. General Interpretation Principle

ROMION does not define interactions as forces between objects.

Instead:
- interactions are **ways relational structure reorganizes**,
- they arise from:
  - loop stabilization,
  - bridge dynamics,
  - boundary activity,
  - projection effects.

Names such as “electromagnetic” or “strong”
are labels applied *after* mapping.

## 3. Electromagnetic Interaction

### 3.1 Phase Mode Interpretation

Electromagnetism maps to:
- a **U(1) phase degree of freedom** on loops and bridges,
- a mode of relational adjustment rather than object exchange.

The photon corresponds to:
- a discrete excitation of this phase mode,
- not to a particle traveling between nodes.

Charge corresponds to:
- persistent phase winding on loop structures.

---

### 3.2 Propagation

Electromagnetic influence propagates via:
- bridge relations,
- preferred relational paths,
- coherence‑weighted transitions.

Apparent propagation speed is emergent
and depends on relational density and tension.

## 4. Strong Interaction

### 4.1 Binding and Confinement

The strong interaction is mapped to:
- high‑stability loop bundles,
- strong overlap penalties,
- increasing cost of separation.

Key features:
- isolating single quark‑like loops is structurally forbidden,
- separation cost grows with structural distance,
- stability favors composite persistence.

No force carrier is required.

---

### 4.2 Color as Degeneracy

“Color” corresponds to:
- internal degeneracy of loop states,
- distinct but equivalent structural roles.

Gluon‑like effects correspond to:
- transformations within degeneracy space,
- conserving composite constraints.

---

## 5. Weak Interaction

### 5.1 Structural Transitions

Weak interaction maps to:
- transformations between loop classes,
- changes in excitation index or orientation,
- reconfiguration of internal degrees of freedom.

Such transitions:
- often reduce stability,
- promote decay,
- change class membership.

---

### 5.2 Chiral Asymmetry

Observed asymmetries are interpreted as:
- inherent asymmetries in allowed reconfigurations,
- orientation‑dependent stabilization,
- projection bias at the CORE/FRACTURE boundary.

No fundamental handedness is postulated.

---

## 6. Higgs‑Like Effects

The Higgs mechanism is interpreted as:
- a **global modification of stabilization cost**,
- not a particle background.

Higgs‑like behavior corresponds to:
- shifts in loop stability μ,
- changes in excitation accessibility,
- altered persistence of structures.

Mass corresponds to:
- cost of maintaining stabilized loops within this regime.

- modification of available relational paths.

Gravitation is not a force,
but a projection of structural constraint.
